\subsection{Dirac's Notation}

Dirac notation was introduced to make \textbf{linear algebra with complex vectors easier, cleaner, and more expressive}, especially for quantum systems.

\highspace
Let's imagine our work only with standard linear algebra notation, where we have to write out all the vectors and matrices in their full form:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Vectors:
    \begin{equation*}
        \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}
            a \\ b
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{equation*}
    \item Conjugate transpose:
    \begin{equation*}
        \mathbf{v}^{\dagger} = \begin{bmatrix}
            \bar{a} & \bar{b}
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{equation*}
    \item Inner product:
    \begin{equation*}
        \mathbf{v}^{\dagger} \mathbf{w} = \bar{a}c + \bar{b}d
    \end{equation*}
    \item Matrix-vector multiplication:
    \begin{equation*}
        M \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}
            m_{11} & m_{12} \\ m_{21} & m_{22}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            a \\ b
        \end{bmatrix}
        =
        \begin{bmatrix}
            m_{11}a + m_{12}b \\ m_{21}a + m_{22}b
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{equation*}
\end{itemize}
This quickly becomes \textbf{cumbersome and hard to read}, especially as we deal with more complex quantum systems. In quantum mechanics we constantly write expressions involving inner products, matrix operations, and state transformations.

\highspace
\textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{check-circle} \textbf{Dirac's idea.}} Paul Dirac said ``\emph{let's give different roles \textbf{different symbols}}''. So he introduced:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \definitionWithSpecificIndex{Kets}{Ket}{} $\ket{v}$ to represent \textbf{column vectors} (quantum states).
    \item  \definitionWithSpecificIndex{Bras}{Bra}{} $\bra{v}$ to represent row vectors (\textbf{conjugate transposes}).
\end{itemize}
Where the operators stay in the middle, and we can write everything in a much cleaner way:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Vectors (\textbf{kets}):
    \begin{equation*}
        \ket{v} = \begin{bmatrix}
            a \\ b
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{equation*}
    Where the entries are complex numbers and usually we \hl{require} $\ket{v}$ to have unit norm (normalized state).
    
    \item Conjugate transpose (\textbf{bra}):
    \begin{equation*}
        \bra{v} = \begin{bmatrix}
            \bar{a} & \bar{b}
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{equation*}
    
    \item Inner product:
    \begin{equation*}
        \braket{v}{w} = \bar{a}c + \bar{b}d
    \end{equation*}
    The result is a scalar (complex number) that tells us how much the two states overlap.
    
    \item Matrix-vector multiplication:
    \begin{equation*}
        M \ket{v} = \begin{bmatrix}
            m_{11} & m_{12} \\ m_{21} & m_{22}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            a \\ b
        \end{bmatrix}
        =
        \begin{bmatrix}
            m_{11}a + m_{12}b \\ m_{21}a + m_{22}b
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{equation*}
    An operator $M$ acting on a state $\ket{v}$ gives us a new state. The result is a new ket.
\end{itemize}
Even if we ignore physics for now, we can see that Dirac's notation is more expressive and easier to read than standard linear algebra notation.

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{question-circle} \textbf{What is \emph{Chaining} in Dirac notation?}}
\end{flushleft}
\textbf{Chaining} means writing multiple operations together in a single expression without needing to write out intermediate steps. For example:
\begin{equation*}
    \bra{x} M \ket{y}
\end{equation*}
It is just a \textbf{compact way to combine linear algebra operations} (matrix multiplication and inner products) in a single expression. It allows us to write complex quantum mechanical expressions in a much cleaner and more intuitive way.

\highspace
Without Dirac notation, we would have to write:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{x}^{\dagger} M \mathbf{y}
\end{equation*}
That is a \textbf{chain of operations}: take the conjugate transpose of $\mathbf{x}$, multiply it by $M$, and then multiply the result by $\mathbf{y}$. With Dirac notation, we can write this in a much more compact and readable way as $\bra{x} M \ket{y}$.

\highspace
To read $\bra{x} M \ket{y}$, we can think of it as:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Start with the ket $\ket{y}$ (a quantum state).
    \item Apply the operator $M$ to $\ket{y}$, which gives us a new state $M \ket{y}$.
    \item Take the inner product of $\bra{x}$ with the new state $M \ket{y}$, which gives us a scalar (complex number) that represents the probability amplitude of transitioning from state $\ket{y}$ to state $\ket{x}$ via the operator $M$. At first, this might seem abstract, but as we go through more examples and applications in quantum mechanics and quantum computing, it will become much clearer how powerful and intuitive Dirac's notation is for describing quantum systems.
\end{enumerate}
