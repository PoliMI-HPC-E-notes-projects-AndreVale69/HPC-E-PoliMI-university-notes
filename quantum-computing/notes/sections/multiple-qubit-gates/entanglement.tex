\subsection{Entanglement}

As discussed on \autopageref{subsubsec:why-entanglement-matters}, we have already seen why entanglement exists and matters. In short, \hl{describing an $n$-qubit quantum state requires an exponential number of parameters, meaning that most multi-qubit states are entangled.}

\highspace
If qubits were independent (separable), we would only need $2n$ real numbers, since each qubit would require only 2 real parameters:
\begin{equation*}
    \ket{v} = a_0 \ket{0} + a_1 \ket{1}
\end{equation*}
Each qubit needs two real parameters, so for $n$ qubits, we would need $2n$ real numbers. However, a general quantum state of $n$ qubits is given by (see \autopageref{eq:general-multiple-qubit-state}):
\begin{equation*}
    \ket{\psi} = \sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} c_k \ket{k}
\end{equation*}
This representation requires $2^n$ complex coefficients (subject to normalization and global phase constraints), corresponding to an exponential number of real degrees of freedom.

\highspace
In conclusion, most multi-qubit states cannot be described as independent qubits. This phenomenon is called \textbf{entanglement} and is a fundamental feature of quantum mechanics with no classical analog.

\begin{definitionbox}[: Entanglement]
    A multi-qubit state is called \definition{entangled} if it \hl{\textbf{cannot} be written as a tensor product} of individual qubit states (i.e., it is not separable). Otherwise, it is called \textbf{separable} or \textbf{unentangled}. Entanglement can be viewed as a ``failure of factorization'', where the state of the whole system cannot be factored into states of its parts.
\end{definitionbox}

\noindent
An important consequence of entanglement is that we cannot assign a separate quantum state to each qubit independently.

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{tools} \textbf{Analysis of a Real Example: Bell States}}
\end{flushleft}
\hl{To better understand the concept of entanglement}, it is useful to \hl{analyze a concrete example.} In particular, we consider a class of two-qubit states known as \textbf{Bell states}, which provide the simplest and most important examples of entangled states. These states cannot be written as a tensor product of individual qubit states, and therefore explicitly demonstrate the impossibility of decomposing certain multi-qubit states into independent subsystems.

\newpage

\begin{definitionbox}[: Bell States]
    The \definition{Bell States} are a set of 4 two-qubit states that form an orthonormal basis of maximally entangled states. They are defined as:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:bell-states}
        \begin{aligned}
            \ket{\Phi^+} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00} + \ket{11}\right) \qquad &\ket{\Phi^-} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00} - \ket{11}\right) \\[.3em]
            \ket{\Psi^+} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{01} + \ket{10}\right) \qquad &\ket{\Psi^-} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{01} - \ket{10}\right)
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
    These states are called \textbf{maximally entangled} because each qubit, when considered individually, is in a completely mixed (maximally uncertain) state. In other words, Bell states are characterized by the fact that \hl{each individual qubit is in a maximally mixed} (\emph{completely random}) state, while the \hl{joint state exhibits perfect correlations between the qubits.}

    \highspace
    This means that when we measure a single qubit of a Bell state, the outcome is completely random. In other words, there is a 50\% chance of getting a 0 and a 50\% chance of getting a 1. Therefore, we cannot say that it is mostly a 0 or a 1. However, once we measure a single qubit, a paradox occurs: even though each qubit alone is random, together they are perfectly correlated. In fact, if we measure the first qubit and get \ket{0}, we know with certainty that the second qubit is also \ket{0} (in the case of $\ket{\Phi^{+}}$ and $\ket{\Phi^{-}}$), or \ket{1} (in the case of $\ket{\Psi^{+}}$ and $\ket{\Psi^{-}}$). Thus, we have \textbf{maximum uncertainty individually but maximum correlation jointly}.
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{examplebox}[: Proof of Entanglement in Bell States]
    After introducing the Bell states, we would like to \hl{proof that decomposing them into independent qubits is impossible.} In other words, we want to show that:
    \begin{equation*}
        \ket{\Phi^+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{11})
    \end{equation*}
    Can be written as:
    \begin{equation*}
        \ket{\Phi^+} = \ket{v_A} \otimes \ket{v_B}
    \end{equation*}
    \begin{proof}
        Let's assume, for the sake of argument, that $\ket{\Phi^+}$ can be written as a tensor product of two single-qubit states:
        \begin{equation*}
            \ket{\Phi^+} = \ket{v_A} \otimes \ket{v_B}
        \end{equation*}
        Where:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $\ket{v_A} = a_0 \ket{0} + a_1 \ket{1}$ is the state of the first qubit.
            \item $\ket{v_B} = b_0 \ket{0} + b_1 \ket{1}$ is the state of the second qubit.
        \end{itemize}
        The tensor product of these two states is:
        \begin{equation*}
            \ket{v_A} \otimes \ket{v_B} = a_0 b_0 \ket{00} + a_0 b_1 \ket{01} + a_1 b_0 \ket{10} + a_1 b_1 \ket{11}
        \end{equation*}
        For this to equal $\ket{\Phi^+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{11})$, we must have:
        \begin{equation*}
            a_0 b_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad a_1 b_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad a_0 b_1 = 0, \quad a_1 b_0 = 0
        \end{equation*}
        But here's the problem, if $a_0 b_1 = 0$, then either $a_0 = 0$ or $b_1 = 0$:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item[\textcolor{Red2}{\faIcon{times}}] Case $a_0 = 0$, then $a_0b_0 = 0$, which \textbf{contradicts} $a_0 b_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ for $\ket{00}$.
            \item[\textcolor{Red2}{\faIcon{times}}] Case $b_1 = 0$, then $a_1 b_1 = 0$, which \textbf{contradicts} $a_1 b_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ for $\ket{11}$.
        \end{itemize}
        Therefore, there is no way to choose $a_0, a_1, b_0, b_1$ such that the above equations hold simultaneously. This means that $\ket{\Phi^+}$ cannot be written as a tensor product of two single-qubit states, and thus it is an entangled state. \hl{All we can say is that these qubits are correlated and cannot be described independently.}
    \end{proof}
\end{examplebox}

\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{question-circle} \textbf{Entanglement: Intrinsic or Basis-Dependent?}}
\end{flushleft}
Having established that Bell states are entangled by showing that they cannot be written as a tensor product of individual qubit states, a natural question arises: \hl{is this property intrinsic to the state, or does it depend on the basis in which the state is expressed?} In other words, could a change of basis make an entangled state appear separable? The answer is no. \textbf{Entanglement} is not a property of the representation, but of the \textbf{structure of the state itself}.

\highspace
In contrast to entanglement, \textbf{superposition depends on the basis we choose}, for example:
\begin{equation*}
    \ket{0} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{+} + \ket{-}\right)
\end{equation*}
This shows that the state \ket{0} is a \textbf{superposition} of \ket{+} and \ket{-} in the \textbf{Hadamard basis}, but it is \textbf{not a superposition in the computational basis}, indeed:
\begin{equation*}
    \ket{0} = 1 \cdot \ket{0} + 0 \cdot \ket{1}
\end{equation*}
It is not a superposition because only one basis vector is present.\footnote{A state is a superposition in a given basis if it is written as a combination of multiple basis vectors with non-zero coefficients.}

\highspace
Differently, \textbf{entanglement} does \textbf{not} depend on basis, because it depends on \textbf{factorization}, not representation. So we can change basis, rewrite the state, but we \textbf{cannot make an entangled state separable}. This prevents a common misunderstanding: one might wonder whether a suitable change of basis could remove entanglement. This is not the case. Entanglement is preserved under local changes of basis, that is, transformations of the form $U_A \otimes U_B$ acting independently on each qubit. Therefore, \hl{entanglement is an intrinsic property of the quantum state and does not depend on the particular representation of each subsystem.}

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{random} \textbf{From States to Operations}}
\end{flushleft}
So far, we have analyzed entanglement as a property of quantum states, showing that certain states cannot be decomposed into independent subsystems. A natural question now arises: \hl{\emph{how can such entangled states be generated in practice?}} In other words, \textbf{which quantum operations are capable of transforming separable states into entangled ones?}

\highspace
The answer lies in a special class of quantum gates, known as \textbf{entangling gates}, which introduce interactions between qubits and allow the creation of correlations that cannot be reduced to independent components.

\highspace
\begin{definitionbox}[: Entangling Gates]
    A multi-qubit gate is called \textbf{entangling} (\definition{Entangling Gates}) if it cannot be written as a tensor product of single-qubit gates. Equivalently, a gate is entangling if there exists at least one separable input state that it transforms into an entangled state.

    \highspace
    Put simply, an \textbf{entangling gate is a gate that makes qubits interact and \underline{can} create entanglement}.

    \highspace
    \textcolor{Red2}{\faIcon{exclamation-triangle} \textbf{Attention:}} Not every input becomes entangled. There exists at least one input that becomes entangled, but not all inputs necessarily do.
\end{definitionbox}

\noindent
In simple terms, an entangling gate is one that can take two or more qubits that are initially uncorrelated (separable) and produce a state where the qubits become correlated in such a way that they cannot be described independently (entangled). For example, the CNOT gate is an entangling gate because it can create Bell states from separable inputs.

\begin{examplebox}[: CNOT as an Entangling Gate]
    Consider the separable state:
    \begin{equation*}
        \ket{+} \otimes \ket{0}
        =
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + \ket{1}) \otimes \ket{0}
        =
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{10})
    \end{equation*}
    This is a separable state, since it is written as a tensor product of two single-qubit states.

    \highspace
    Now apply a CNOT gate (with the first qubit as control and the second as target, \autopageref{sec:controlled-not-cnot-gate}):
    \begin{equation*}
        \text{CNOT} \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{10}) \right)
        =
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{11})
    \end{equation*}
    The resulting state is the Bell state $\ket{\Phi^+}$, which is entangled. Therefore, the \hl{CNOT gate is an \textbf{entangling gate}.}
\end{examplebox}

\begin{examplebox}[: CNOT Does Not Always Create Entanglement]
    Not all inputs to an entangling gate produce entangled states. Consider the separable state:
    \begin{equation*}
        \ket{0} \otimes \ket{0} = \ket{00}
    \end{equation*}
    Applying the CNOT gate:
    \begin{equation*}
        \text{CNOT}(\ket{00}) = \ket{00}
    \end{equation*}
    The state remains separable.

    \highspace
    This shows that an entangling gate does not necessarily produce entanglement for every input. However, it is called \textbf{entangling} because there exists at least one separable input state that it transforms into an entangled state.
\end{examplebox}

\begin{examplebox}[: Bell-state generator]
    Exists a simple quantum circuit that produces \textbf{different Bell states depending on the input}. So, for the four input states $\ket{00}$, $\ket{01}$, $\ket{10}$, and $\ket{11}$, the circuit creates the four Bell states $\ket{\Phi^+}$, $\ket{\Phi^-}$, $\ket{\Psi^+}$, and $\ket{\Psi^-}$ respectively. This circuit consists of a Hadamard gate followed by a CNOT gate:
    \begin{center}
        \begin{quantikz}[slice all]
            \lstick{$\ket{v_A}$} & \gate{H} & \ctrl{1} & \\
            \lstick{$\ket{v_B}$} &          & \targ{}  &
        \end{quantikz}
        $\quad$
        $(\underbrace{\text{CNOT}}_{2} \cdot \underbrace{\left(H \otimes I\right)}_{1}) \ket{\left(\ket{v_A} \otimes \ket{v_B}\right)}$
    \end{center}
    This circuit is a \textbf{Bell-state generator} because it can produce any of the four Bell states from the appropriate input.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Input $\ket{00}$ produces $\ket{\Phi^+}$:
        \begin{center}
            \begin{quantikz}
                \lstick{$\ket{0}$} & \gate{H} & \ctrl{1} & \\
                \lstick{$\ket{0}$} &          & \targ{}  &
            \end{quantikz}
            $\quad$
            $\ket{\Phi^+} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00} + \ket{11}\right)$
        \end{center}
        \item Input $\ket{01}$ produces $\ket{\Phi^-}$:
        \begin{center}
            \begin{quantikz}
                \lstick{$\ket{0}$} & \gate{H} & \ctrl{1} & \\
                \lstick{$\ket{1}$} &          & \targ{}  &
            \end{quantikz}
            $\quad$
            $\ket{\Phi^-} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00} - \ket{11}\right)$
        \end{center}
        \item Input $\ket{10}$ produces $\ket{\Psi^+}$:
        \begin{center}
            \begin{quantikz}
                \lstick{$\ket{1}$} & \gate{H} & \ctrl{1} & \\
                \lstick{$\ket{0}$} &          & \targ{}  &
            \end{quantikz}
            $\quad$
            $\ket{\Psi^+} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{01} + \ket{10}\right)$
        \end{center}
        \item Input $\ket{11}$ produces $\ket{\Psi^-}$:
        \begin{center}
            \begin{quantikz}
                \lstick{$\ket{1}$} & \gate{H} & \ctrl{1} & \\
                \lstick{$\ket{1}$} &          & \targ{}  &
            \end{quantikz}
            $\quad$
            $\ket{\Psi^-} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{01} - \ket{10}\right)$
        \end{center}
    \end{itemize}
\end{examplebox}

\begin{examplebox}[: Bell States in the Hadamard Basis]
    In this exercise, we express the Bell state $\ket{\Phi^{+}}$ in the Hadamard basis instead of the computational basis.

    \begin{proof}[Bell state $\ket{\Phi^{+}}$ in the Hadamard basis]
        Given:
        \begin{equation*}
            \ket{\Phi^{+}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00} + \ket{11}\right)
        \end{equation*}
        We aim to re-express this state using the Hadamard basis $\left\{\ket{+}, \ket{-}\right\}$, where:
        \begin{equation*}
            \ket{+} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{0} + \ket{1}\right)
            \qquad
            \ket{-} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{0} - \ket{1}\right)
        \end{equation*}
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item \textbf{Invert the definitions}. To convert from the computational basis to the Hadamard basis, we invert the above equations to express \ket{0} and \ket{1} in terms of \ket{+} and \ket{-}:
            \begin{equation*}
                \ket{0} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{+} + \ket{-}\right)
                \qquad
                \ket{1} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{+} - \ket{-}\right)
            \end{equation*}

            \item \textbf{Substitute into \ket{00} and \ket{{11}}}. We compute the tensor products using the expression above.
            \begin{itemize}
                \item Compute \ket{00}:
                \begin{equation*}
                    \begin{aligned}
                        \ket{00} =& \ket{0} \otimes \ket{0} \\[.3em]
                        =& \left( \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{+} + \ket{-}\right) \right)
                            \otimes
                            \left( \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{+} + \ket{-}\right) \right) \\[.3em]
                        =& \,\dfrac{1}{2} \left(\ket{++} + \ket{+-} + \ket{-+} + \ket{--}\right)
                    \end{aligned}
                \end{equation*}
                \item Compute \ket{11}:
                \begin{equation*}
                    \begin{aligned}
                        \ket{11} =& \ket{1} \otimes \ket{1} \\[.3em]
                        =& \left( \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{+} - \ket{-}\right) \right)
                            \otimes
                            \left( \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{+} - \ket{-}\right) \right) \\[.3em]
                        =& \dfrac{1}{2} \left(\ket{++} - \ket{+-} - \ket{-+} + \ket{--}\right)
                    \end{aligned}
                \end{equation*}
            \end{itemize}

            \item \textbf{Add the two to get $\ket{\Phi^{+}}$}. Now add the two results:
            \begin{equation*}
                \ket{\Phi^{+}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00} + \ket{11}\right)
            \end{equation*}
            Substituting:
            \begin{equation*}
                \begin{aligned}
                    \ket{\Phi^{+}} =& \,\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left[
                        \dfrac{1}{2} \left( \ket{++} + \ket{+-} + \ket{-+} + \ket{--} \right)
                    \right. \\[.3em]
                    & \left.
                    \phantom{\,\dfrac{1}{2}}\hspace{1.2em} + \dfrac{1}{2} \left(\ket{++} - \ket{+-} - \ket{-+} + \ket{--}\right)
                    \right] \\[.3em]
                    =& \,\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} \left( 2\ket{++} + 0\ket{+-} + 0\ket{-+} + 2\ket{--} \right) \\[.3em]
                    =& \,\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} \left( 2\ket{++} + 2\ket{--} \right) \\[.3em]
                    =& \,\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{++} + \ket{--}\right)
                \end{aligned}
            \end{equation*}
        \end{enumerate}
        Thus, in the Hadamard basis, we have:
        \begin{equation*}
            \ket{\Phi^{+}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{++} + \ket{--}\right)
        \end{equation*}
        Which is a Bell-like state expressed in the Hadamard basis instead of the computational basis.
    \end{proof}
\end{examplebox}
