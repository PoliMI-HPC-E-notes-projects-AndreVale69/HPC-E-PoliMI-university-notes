\subsubsection{Pauli-X (NOT) Gate}\label{sec:pauli-x-not-gate}

Before focusing specifically on the Pauli-X gate, it is helpful to understand the broader context of the \definition{Pauli Gates}. The Pauli gates -- X, Y, and Z -- form a set of fundamental single-qubit operations that:
\begin{itemize}
   \item Are \textbf{unitary} ($U^{\dagger} U = U U^{\dagger} = I$) and \textbf{Hermitian} ($U = U^{\dagger}$), making them both reversible and observable-related.
   \item Represent \textbf{rotations of $\pi$} radians around the principal axes ($x$, $y$, and $z$) of the Bloch sphere.
   \item Generate the algebra of single-qubit operations and are widely used in quantum algorithms, error correction, and quantum circuit design.
   \item Have eigenvalues $\pm 1$ and satisfy the property:
   \begin{equation*}
      X^{2} = Y^{2} = Z^{2} = I
   \end{equation*}
   \hl{Meaning that applying the same Pauli gate twice returns the qubit to its original state.}
\end{itemize}
Among these, the \textbf{Pauli-X gate} is the simplest to understand and provides a direct bridge between classical and quantum logic operations.

\highspace
The \definitionWithSpecificIndex{Pauli-X Gate}{Single Qubit Gates: Pauli-X Gate}{}, often called the \textbf{quantum NOT gate}, is one of the \emph{fundamental} single-qubit quantum gates. It is denoted $X$ and \textbf{corresponds to the} $\sigma_{x}$ \textbf{Pauli matrix} in quantum mechanics. This gate \hl{\textbf{flips the state} of a qubit, \textbf{similar to how a classical NOT gate inverts a bit}.}

\highspace
\begin{remarkbox}[: Pauli Matrix]
   In quantum mechanics, the \definition{Pauli matrices} are \textbf{a set of three $2 \times 2$ Hermitian and unitary matrices} that represent \textbf{spin operators} for spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ particles and are widely used in quantum computing to describe single-qubit gates. They are:
   \begin{equation*}
      \begin{array}{rcl}
         \sigma_{x} =
         \begin{pmatrix}
            0 & 1 \\
            1 & 0
         \end{pmatrix} \quad
         \sigma_{y} =
         \begin{pmatrix}
            0 & -i \\
            i & 0
         \end{pmatrix} \quad
         \sigma_{z} =
         \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 0 \\
            0 & -1
         \end{pmatrix}
      \end{array}
   \end{equation*}
   Key properties:
   \begin{itemize}
      \item Hermitian: $\sigma_{i}^{\dagger} = \sigma_{i}$ (observable-related)
      \item Unitary: $\sigma_{i}^{\dagger} \sigma_{i} = I$ (reversible transformations)
      \item Involution: $\sigma_{i}^{2} = I$
      \item Non-commuting: $\left[\sigma_{i}, \sigma_{j}\right] = 2i \varepsilon_{ijk} \sigma_{k}$ (with $\varepsilon$ the Levi-Civita symbol)
   \end{itemize}
\end{remarkbox}

\newpage

\begin{flushleft}
   \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{square-root-alt} \textbf{Matrix Representation}}
\end{flushleft}
\begin{equation}
   X = \sigma_{x} =
   \begin{pmatrix}
      0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0
   \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
This \textbf{matrix swaps} the $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$ \textbf{basis states}:
\begin{equation}
   X\ket{0} = \ket{1} \hspace{2em} \text{and} \hspace{2em} X\ket{1} = \ket{0}
\end{equation}

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
   \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{book} \textbf{Action on a General Qubit}}
\end{flushleft}
Let's consider a qubit in the general state:
\begin{equation*}
   \ket{\psi} = a\ket{0} + b\ket{1}
\end{equation*}
Applying the $X$ gate:
\begin{equation*}
   X\ket{\psi} = a X \ket{0} + b X \ket{1} = a \ket{1} + b \ket{0} = b \ket{0} + a \ket{1}
\end{equation*}
As a result, the \textbf{amplitudes} $a$ and $b$ are \textbf{swapped}.

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
   \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{list-ul} \textbf{Key Properties of X Gate}}
\end{flushleft}
The Pauli-X gate satisfies several important mathematical properties:
\begin{enumerate}
   \item \important{Unitary}. The X gate is unitary, meaning it preserves the inner product and is reversible:
   \begin{equation*}
      X X^{\dagger} = I
   \end{equation*}


   \item \important{Hermitian}. The X gate is also Hermitian, which means it is equal to its own conjugate transpose:
   \begin{equation*}
      X = X^{\dagger}
   \end{equation*}


   \item \important{Self-Inverse}. The X gate is its own inverse, which means that \textbf{applying it twice returns the qubit to its original state}:
   \begin{equation*}
      X^{-1} = X \quad \Rightarrow \quad X^{2} = I
   \end{equation*}
\end{enumerate}

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
   \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{question-circle} \textbf{Geometric Interpretation: Rotation on the Bloch Sphere}}
\end{flushleft}
The Pauli-X gate corresponds to a \textbf{rotation around the $x$-axis by $\pi$ radians ($180\degree$)} on the Bloch sphere, denoted as $X = R_{x}(\pi)$.

\highspace
\textbf{To understand} this, recall the \textbf{general qubit state in spherical coordinates}:
\begin{equation*}
   \ket{\psi} = \cos\left(\dfrac{\theta}{2}\right) \ket{0} + e^{i \phi} \sin\left(\dfrac{\theta}{2}\right) \ket{1}
\end{equation*}
After a $\pi$ rotation around the $x$-axis:
\begin{itemize}
   \item $\theta \rightarrow \pi - \theta$
   \item $\phi \rightarrow -\phi$ 
\end{itemize}
This \textbf{rotation mirrors the qubit across the $x$-axis}, flipping its position between $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$, and adjusting the phase accordingly.

\begin{flushleft}
   \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{book} \textbf{Eigenstates}}
\end{flushleft}
In linear algebra, an \textbf{eigenvector} of a matrix (or linear operator) $A$ is a non-zero vector $\ket{v}$ that, when the operator is applied, changes only by a \textbf{scalar factor} called the \textbf{eigenvalue} $\lambda$:
\begin{equation*}
   A\ket{v} = \lambda \ket{v}
\end{equation*}
In quantum mechanics, we use the term \definition{Eigenstate} instead of eigenvector. So, an eigenstate of an operator $U$ satisfies:
\begin{equation*}
   U\ket{v} = \lambda \ket{v}
\end{equation*}
An \textbf{eigenstate} is a state that remains \textbf{unchanged in direction} after the operation is applied, except only its \textbf{magnitude} or \textbf{phase} may change by a factor of $\lambda$. For the Pauli-X gate, solving the eigenvalue equation:
\begin{equation*}
   X\ket{v} = \lambda \ket{v}
\end{equation*}
Yields two eigenstates:
\begin{equation}
   \ket{+} = \dfrac{\ket{0} + \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}} \quad \text{with eigenvalue} \quad +1 \Rightarrow X\ket{+} = +1 \cdot \ket{+} = \ket{+}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
   \ket{-} = \dfrac{\ket{0} - \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}} \quad \text{with eigenvalue} \quad -1 \Rightarrow X\ket{-} = -1 \cdot \ket{-} = -\ket{-}
\end{equation}

\highspace
\begin{examplebox}[: Measurement in the X-basis]
   For example, if a qubit is in the state $\ket{x}$ and we measure it in the X-basis, the outcome is $1$ with probability $1$.
   \begin{proof}
      We want to show that if the qubit is in the state:
      \begin{equation*}
         \ket{x} = \dfrac{\ket{0} + \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
      \end{equation*}
      And we measure it in the X-basis $\left\{ \ket{+}, \ket{-} \right\}$, then the \hl{outcome is $+1$ with probability $1$}.

      Measuring in the X-basis means using the eigenstates of the Pauli-X operator:
      \begin{equation*}
         \ket{+} = \dfrac{\ket{0} + \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad \ket{-} = \dfrac{\ket{0} - \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
      \end{equation*}
      These correspond to the new possible outcomes: $+1$ for $\ket{+}$ and $-1$ for $\ket{-}$.

      If the system is in state $\ket{\psi}$, the probability of obtaining the outcome corresponding to basis state $\ket{u}$ is:
      \begin{equation*}
         P(u) = \left|\braket{u}{\psi}\right|^{2}
      \end{equation*}
      In our case, the system is in state $\ket{\psi} = \ket{+}$, so:
      \begin{itemize}
         \item Probability of outcome $+1$ (measuring $\ket{+}$):
         \begin{equation*}
            P(+1) = \left|\braket{+}{+}\right|^{2}
         \end{equation*}
         \item Probability of outcome $-1$ (measuring $\ket{-}$):
         \begin{equation*}
            P(-1) = \left|\braket{-}{+}\right|^{2}
         \end{equation*}
      \end{itemize}
      Since every normalized state has inner product 1 with itself, we have:
      \begin{equation*}
         \braket{+}{+} = 1
      \end{equation*}
      Therefore:
      \begin{equation*}
         P(+) = \left|\braket{+}{+}\right|^{2} = \left|1\right|^{2} = 1
      \end{equation*}
      So the probability of getting $+1$ is $1$.

      If we calculate the probability of getting $-1$, first write the bras and kets:
      \begin{equation*}
         \ket{+} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{0} + \ket{1}\right), \quad \ket{-} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{0} - \ket{1}\right)
      \end{equation*}
      Changing the ket $\ket{+}$ to a bra:
      \begin{equation*}
         \bra{-} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\bra{0} - \bra{1}\right)
      \end{equation*}
      Now we can compute the inner product:
      \begin{align*}
         \braket{-}{+} =& \: \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\bra{0} - \bra{1}\right) \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\ket{0} + \ket{1}\right) \\[.2em]
         \downarrow     & \: \text{ bring out the factors} \\[.2em]
         =              & \: \dfrac{1}{2} \left(\bra{0} - \bra{1}\right) \left(\ket{0} + \ket{1}\right) \\[.2em]
         \downarrow     & \: \text{ expand the product} \\[.2em]
         =              & \: \dfrac{1}{2} \left(\braket{0}{0} + \braket{0}{1} - \braket{1}{0} - \braket{1}{1}\right) \\[.2em]
         \downarrow     & \: \text{ use orthonormality ($\braket{0}{0} = \braket{1}{1} = 1$, $\braket{0}{1} = \braket{1}{0} = 0$)} \\[.2em]
         =              & \: \dfrac{1}{2} \left(1 + 0 - 0 - 1\right) \\[.2em]
         =              & \: 0
      \end{align*}
      Therefore:
      \begin{equation*}
         P(-1) = \left|\braket{-}{+}\right|^{2} = \left|0\right|^{2} = 0
      \end{equation*}
      So the probability of getting $-1$ is $0$.
   \end{proof}
\end{examplebox}
