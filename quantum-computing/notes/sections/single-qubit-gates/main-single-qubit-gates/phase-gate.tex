\subsubsection{Phase Gate (S)}\label{subsubsection: Phase Gate S}

The \definitionWithSpecificIndex{Phase Gate (S)}{Single Qubit Gates: Phase Gate (S)}{} is a single-qubit gate that \textbf{adds a phase shift of $\frac{\pi}{2}$ ($90\degree$)} \textbf{to the amplitude of the $\ket{1}$ state}. It \textbf{leaves $\ket{0}$ unchanged}, like the Pauli-Z gate, but instead of a sign flip ($\pi$ phase), it \textbf{introduces a complex phase factor $i$}.

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{square-root-alt} \textbf{Matrix Representation}}
\end{flushleft}
\begin{equation}
    S = \begin{pmatrix}
        1 & 0 \\ 0 & i
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{book} \textbf{Action on a General Qubit}}
\end{flushleft}
For a state:
\begin{equation*}
    \ket{\psi} = a\ket{0} + b\ket{1}
\end{equation*}
Apply $S$:
\begin{equation*}
    S\ket{\psi} = a\ket{0} + ib\ket{1}
\end{equation*}
Only the $\ket{1}$ \textbf{amplitude gains a phase of} $i$, affecting interference but \textbf{not measurement probabilities} in the computational basis.
\begin{equation*}
    S\ket{0} = \ket{0} \hspace{1em} \text{(unchanged)}, \hspace{2em} S\ket{1} = i\ket{1} \hspace{1em} \left(\text{phase } + \frac{\pi}{2}\right)
\end{equation*}

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{question-circle} \textbf{Geometric Interpretation: Rotation around $z$-axis $\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)$}}
\end{flushleft}
On the Bloch sphere, $S$ performs a rotation of $\frac{\pi}{2}$ radians around the $z$-axis. This rotation \textbf{shifts the phase angle} $\phi \rightarrow \phi + \frac{\pi}{2}$, altering \textbf{how the qubit interferes} in later operations, especially when \textbf{Hadamard gates} are involved. In \autoref{fig:phase-s-gate}, we see how $S$ transforms the state $\ket{+}$ (on the $x$-axis) into $\ket{i}$ (on the $y$-axis) through a $\frac{\pi}{2}$ rotation around the $z$-axis, demonstrating the phase shift's effect on the qubit's state.

\highspace
\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{exchange-alt} \textbf{Relationship to Pauli-Z}}
\end{flushleft}
$S$ is often called the \textbf{square root of Z} because applying it twice yields the Pauli-Z gate: $S = \sqrt{Z}$. Pauli-Z is a \textbf{full phase flip}:
\begin{equation*}
    e^{i\pi} = -1
\end{equation*}
The $S$ gate is a \textbf{partial phase shift}:
\begin{equation*}
    e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}} = i
\end{equation*}
So:
\begin{equation*}
    Z = e^{i \pi \cdot \ket{1}\bra{1}} \quad\text{and}\quad S = e^{i \frac{\pi}{2} \cdot \ket{1}\bra{1}}
\end{equation*}
Same transformation, but different phase angles. Let's verify $S^{2} = Z$:
\begin{equation*}
    S \cdot S = 
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1 & 0 \\ 0 & i
    \end{pmatrix}
    \cdot
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1 & 0 \\ 0 & i
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1 & 0 \\ 0 & i^{2}
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1
    \end{pmatrix}
    = Z
\end{equation*}

\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{book} \textbf{Eigenvalues and Eigenstates}}
\end{flushleft}
The Phase gate $S$ is diagonal in the computational basis, so its eigenstates are $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$:
\begin{equation*}
    S\ket{0} = 1 \cdot \ket{0}, \hspace{2em} S\ket{1} = i \cdot \ket{1}
\end{equation*}
Thus, the eigenvalues of $S$ are:
\begin{equation*}
    \lambda_{0} = 1, \hspace{2em} \lambda_{1} = i
\end{equation*}
This means that $\ket{0}$ remains unchanged, while $\ket{1}$ acquires a phase factor $i = e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}$.

\begin{flushleft}
    \textcolor{Green3}{\faIcon{list-ul} \textbf{Key Properties}}
\end{flushleft}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Unitary}: $S^{\dagger} = S^{-1}$, so normalization is preserved.
    
    \item \textbf{Not Hermitian}: $S^{\dagger} \neq S$, therefore $S$ is not an observable, it is only a transformation.
    
    \item \textbf{Inverse}: 
    \begin{equation*}
        S^{-1} = S^{\dagger} = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 0 \\ 0 & -i
        \end{pmatrix}
    \end{equation*}
    
    \item \textbf{Relation with Pauli-Z}: $S^{2} = Z$, meaning that $S$ is the square root of the Pauli-Z gate.
    
    \item \textbf{Periodicity}: $S^{4} = I$, since $i^{4} = 1$.
    
    \item \textbf{Phase Shift}: $S$ applies a relative phase of $\frac{\pi}{2}$ to the $\ket{1}$ component.
\end{enumerate}
